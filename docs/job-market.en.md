# How to Break Into Tech

**Things you wanted to ask but never did.**

At a Global Diversity CFP Day event organized by Globant, focused on learning how to propose and give tech conference talks, three topics came up that I actually wanted to talk about:

- How to sell smoke with Digital Transformation: Fear as a Service.
- When is a bot actually a bot? An explanation for my 5-year-old niece.
- How to enter the job market as a recent graduate.

The third one is something people ask me about constantly: **"I'm about to graduate. What can I do?"**

---

## Who am I?

My name is Luciano Adonis (yes, that's my last name, not my first), I'm 22, and I have 3 years of experience. Self-proclaimed young DevOps engineer, currently working as a Google Cloud Platform architect. Part of my day-to-day involves managing infrastructure for a well-known airline.

This post is about what we've all been through: looking for your first real job.

## Do you want others to decide for you, or do you want to decide yourself?

![](/docs/images/job-market-1.png)

By my second year studying Systems Infrastructure Engineering, I reached a conclusion: nobody was going to move a finger for me, especially if I wasn't moving first. At that point, I could only accept whatever came my way, and that wasn't what I wanted.

**Settling is not an option.**

![](/docs/images/job-market-2.png)

One of the best professors I ever had mentioned DevOps briefly in class and left it at "look it up if you want, we're moving on." I was the only one who actually did. I looked into what the methodology proposed, liked it, and decided that was where I wanted to go.

With a lot of improvisation, I had to follow that path without a real reference point to identify with.

One of my first realizations while studying on my own was that I couldn't become a DevOps engineer without understanding developers. I had to get out and find people to learn from.

## Three difficulty levels

![](/docs/images/job-market-3.png)

To avoid recommending something that doesn't fit your situation, I split things into three levels:

**Just getting by:** a low-demand job that you could automate yourself out of, but don't want to. You tend to realize later that you didn't actually gain useful experience. Doesn't necessarily mean low pay — I know cases where people were paid well but learned nothing.

**Learning:** a normal job where you're at a level that matches your current knowledge base. Pay tends to be market average, and in the best case scenario, you can grow over time.

**Challenging yourself:** this is where I aimed, knowing it could be painful. As I overcame challenges, I realized this level was right for me. But I don't recommend it unless you're willing to invest your energy into doing what you're passionate about. It requires you to either already have advanced skills or genuinely want to develop them.

## What you're looking for: What can I work on?

Where do you find a good build for yourself? A great way to identify what to reinforce, develop, and master is to research. Good sources:

- **Job boards (GetOnBoard, LinkedIn):** looking at job requirements gives you a clear view of what the market actually needs.
- **Someone who's been there:** a person who went through the same path can save you a lot of circles.
- **Blogs and Medium:** people sharing their experiences, stories, and learning paths.
- **Courses and prep content:** Linux Academy, Udemy, bootcamps. Filter by reviews.

Think of it like building a character in an RPG. The way you distribute your stats will determine which builds you're good at.

## What you study: Does your degree matter?

![](/docs/images/job-market-4.png)

Even in tech, which tends to value initiative, passion, and skills, where you studied still factors into the evaluation.

Your degree is your knowledge base. You can always reinforce it or build your own. If you've experienced implicit discrimination because of where you studied, the good news is that passion can overcome a lot of that. In most cases, it comes down to two questions:

- Do you know it or not?
- Do you want to learn it or not?

## Networking: How do I learn from others?

![](/docs/images/job-market-5.png)

Events aren't just for expanding your contact network. They're environments where you can absorb experience from others.

A few recommendations:

- **BeerJS:** the Chile edition organized by devsChile, covers beer and tech (not just JavaScript).
- **MeetUps:** events of all kinds, usually with food.
- **Notable ones:** AWSome Day, Google Cloud Summit, RedHat, SoCraTes, Starsconf, 9punto5.

Show up with the mindset of learning and connecting. It's not just about the stickers, but those are nice too.

## Communities

When you start looking for people to talk to about shared topics, communities become important. They usually run on Slack.

Of all the ones I've been part of, **devsChile** is the most organized and functional. Great for jokes and with a large number of active members who are genuinely skilled. Don't be afraid to participate or ask questions. Not knowing something or being new won't change how they treat you.

## Added value: What can I do before applying?

![](/docs/images/job-market-6.png)

Certifications are good, but their value is tied to the experience you can only get by working.

For developers, personal projects function as credentials. For DevOps, it's a mix of both.

One often overlooked source of added value: a **good internship**. It's hard to find one that works around class schedules, but if you can orient it toward what you actually want to do, it makes a real difference. My internship put me so far ahead of my peers that I spent the rest of my degree going to classes just for attendance.

## Recruiters: Should I use LinkedIn?

![](/docs/images/job-market-7.png)

Yes, as a window to display your skills and maintain a contact network.

To improve your visibility: use the role you want as your headline. Enable "open to work" and stay active so you show up in searches.

Summarize your experience attractively in a few lines, letting the "show more" trigger so people have to click to keep reading.

On skills: keep them relevant to what you do. Ten validations of "Microsoft Office" and two of the technology you actually work with sends the wrong message.

## Interviews: What should I expect?

![](/docs/images/job-market-8.png)

You're competing against people with more experience who are asking for less money. Prioritize learning over salary at the start.

The typical flow:

1. Company presentation
2. Tell us about yourself
3. What do you like doing?
4. Where did you study?
5. What experience do you have?

Depending on the company, there may be a technical evaluation, either as a take-home or done in the same interview.

What's worth prioritizing before money is knowledge. It will bring you more money in both the short and long term.

## How to raise a Junior

![](/docs/images/job-market-9.png)

Being in charge of a Junior is like being a parent: they won't thank you, and it'll test your patience. Just remember we were all Juniors once.

There's nothing better than giving someone the opportunity and the tools to prove themselves.

---

## How to be a good Junior

![](/docs/images/job-market-10.png)

The mistakes I made and see most often:

**Asking too much:** having a Senior nearby is like having a live Google search. You need to figure things out yourself and only ask when it's something specific to the business context or when you've genuinely exhausted your own attempts.

**Not asking enough:** also a mistake. If you're in a conversation and didn't understand something, ask. The skill is knowing when the right moment is.

**Not controlling your enthusiasm:** people can empathize with how excited you are to learn and talk about work all day. But they've already been through that phase. You don't have to make everything about work.
