Alright. You've prepared. You slept well, your speech is ready, your camera works. Now comes the actual interview.

I've split what follows into phases, because a technical interview in English isn't a monolithic block. It has moments. And each moment has its rules.

---

## Phase One — Getting to know each other

After the pleasantries ("how are you," "nice to meet you," etc.), comes the introduction phase. Usually the interviewer describes the technologies they use and what they're looking for, setting the tone.

Based on my experience, there are three common scenarios:

**Rarely:** The interviewer starts from scratch, asking direct questions and increasing difficulty gradually based on your answers. This rarely happens because you'd need the meeting to last an hour, and the average is 30 minutes.

**Sometimes:** The interviewer starts by asking about the technologies they use, expecting a similar level or at least decent answers. I like this type of interview because it's faster and you can actually learn from it.

**Usually:** The interviewer asks about your experience and builds questions based on what you share, to gauge your level and skills.

This works if your knowledge overlaps at least 50-70% with what they do. It's harder to interrogate someone about something the interviewer doesn't work with themselves. But if they're willing to listen, you can find common ground based on similar approaches like troubleshooting, network configs, etc.

---

## Phase Two — What you say is what you'll get

By now you're more comfortable, the nerves have settled, and the interviewer has a sense of what you bring.

If your structured story about your experience was interesting enough, the interviewer will likely start asking what you expect from the role. That's a good sign.

If not... well, improvise. You did what you could.

The key thing about this phase: the conversation becomes more natural. If you prepared your story and highlights well, this part flows. If you prepared nothing, it shows. A lot.

---

## Bonus Phase — The Feeling

This is the phase most people ignore. And it's probably where more interviews are won or lost without you even realizing it.

A lot of times someone just says "I'd like to work with them" or simply "I like them," and that's enough.

If you're as charismatic as I am, you don't need to read this. If not, keep going. *(It's a joke. More or less.)*

### What interviewers are actually watching

Interviewers watch and evaluate how you react to things. They're quietly asking themselves things like:

- "How did they react when they didn't understand something or couldn't answer?"
- "Did they miss the question and not want to admit it?"
- "Did they hold up under pressure?"
- "Look at them going in circles, I just asked how they were doing..."

It's easy to overlook, but companies are looking for someone to actually work with. The interview gives them a glimpse of that person's character, and they're going to be stuck with you for a good while.

It's not about not being a liability. It's about not making things harder for everyone else.

### Speed matters

If you take minutes to answer simple questions, or you go off on tangents without landing anywhere, that sends a pretty clear message to the interviewer about how you work.

### Engagement makes the difference

Especially if the interviewer is the person you'd be working with directly.

If you don't know something, that's okay. Depending on the interviewer, they might guide you toward an answer or something close. You can recover points by explaining your reasoning and keeping it in the conversation.

It's not about giving up before you answer. It's about actually trying to work through it.

### Troubleshooting as a skill

Finding a piece of code that does what you need is relatively easy. Solving problems specific to a company's particular environment is not.

A former coworker once told me something like:

> "Our jobs could be done with scripts and automation. We're paid because we can solve problems and because of our ability to troubleshoot and adapt to new issues."

The takeaway: when we solve complex problems or architecture challenges, we show how we think and how we operate. And that's what they're evaluating in this phase, even if they don't say it explicitly.

---

## Final Phase — The close

At this point the interview wraps up and the interviewer will either tell you the next steps or close the door in your face. Either way, everything is a learning experience.

### If you get rejected

It hurts. But it's learning. Ask for feedback if you can. Sometimes the rejection has nothing to do with you: timing, budget, they found someone internally, or it just wasn't the match they were looking for.

What you can do: note which questions you couldn't answer, which parts felt weak, and work on those before the next one.

### If you get accepted

Congratulations. Now the real work begins.

Don't relax too much. The interview is the entrance, not the goal.

---

## Visual summary of the phases

- **Phase One:** Pleasantries → Introductions → Interview tone set.
- **Phase Two:** Questions based on your experience → Deeper conversation → Your level gets measured.
- **Bonus Phase:** How you react → Your engagement → Whether you'd be good to work with.
- **Final Phase:** Next steps or "thanks, we'll be in touch" → Feedback → Learn from the experience.
