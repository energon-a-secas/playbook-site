# The Meta Changed — Part 1

**Preparation, mindset, and what you need to know before the call.**

A while back I wrote these notes for coworkers. They were an internal guide, semi-informal, on how to prepare for technical interviews with clients in English. I kept them stored away because the context was very specific to the company I was at.

But the meta changed.

---

The tech market shifted. There were layoffs, restructurings, and suddenly people who had never had to interview in English found themselves looking for work abroad. Or people who were already working internationally but had always been lucky enough to get projects without a heavy selection process.

So I thought: these notes might help. They're not perfect, they don't cover everything, and some parts are very opinionated. But if you're about to interview in English for a technical role, you'll at least have a foundation.

I originally wrote them in English for a specific corporate context. I've adapted and generalized them to be useful beyond one particular company.

This is **Part 1** of a three-part series:

- **Part 1 (this post):** Preparation, mindset, and what you should know before the interview.
- **Part 2:** The interview phases. What happens, when, and how to navigate them.
- **Part 3:** Technical question reference. Cloud, containers, pipelines, IaC, and more.

## Preparation — what you need to sort out first

This sounds obvious, but the number of people who walk into an interview completely unprepared is staggering. And I'm not talking about studying 200 pages of documentation. I mean the basics.

### Know yourself

In the first ten minutes of any interview, you'll need to summarize what you've been doing for the last few years. Your process, achievements, challenges, and how you solved them.

If you can't explain your own experience clearly and concisely, nobody else is going to do it for you.

### Prepare a speech

This can feel awkward at first, especially if you're not used to speaking in English or just not used to being interviewed. But you don't want to improvise. At least not until you're comfortable talking about your experience and can adapt to what the interviewer wants to hear.

Over time you'll learn to read the room. You'll see the interviewers' expressions and know when you're losing them because you're going off on tangents. So having prepared highlights is key. They're talking points at the end of the day.

### Know your tools

Be aware of the technologies and methodologies you've used. You don't want to walk into an interview to discuss something you didn't fully understand from your last job.

Knowledge gaps show up when your answer to a question is "yes, but that was handled by another team." That's understandable, but it doesn't justify being completely foreign to certain topics. At least at a surface level, you should know how things work.

### Sleep well

I follow this rule for everything: **"Studying isn't worth more than my sleep."**

I figured out early on that being fully awake and rested gives you more of an advantage than cramming until the last minute. If you're tired, it shows. If you're awake and calm, that shows too.

### Have your setup ready

Always have your computer configured and ready. Camera, microphone, the video call app they use, all tested beforehand. You don't want to spend the first five minutes of an interview saying "can you hear me?" while hunting for an audio driver.

## What you need to know — the general context

Before getting into the interview phases, some things need to be clear. This applies regardless of the level you're applying for.

### Experience > general knowledge

Practical experience with tools is worth more than general knowledge. At semi-senior level, knowing that something exists is enough. At senior level, that's a given. General knowledge comes separately.

### Certifications — the uncomfortable truth

Certifications are good. But at the Senior level, they're enough to skip the easy questions. Nothing more.

It's hard to hear for people who worked hard to get one, but the reality is: a certification tells me you have time to study, good memory, and probably a subscription to some learning platform.

My policy is to certify in things I'm actively working with and have enough experience to back up. Otherwise I'd be approaching everything with just memorization and cramming.

But this varies by person. If your approach is "I want to learn more about X technology and I'm using this as a challenge to find related work," that's perfectly valid.

---

## Semi-Senior vs Senior — the difference

**At Semi-Senior level:**
- Certifications count as an extension of knowledge and proof that you care about continuing to learn.
- The goal is to verify what you know, more than what you've done.
- Side projects help expand your knowledge beyond your current company's stack.

**At Senior level:**
- Practical experience is what matters.
- You're expected to explain decisions, not just execute tasks.
- Questions are more open-ended and situational.
- You'll need to demonstrate judgment, not just knowledge.

## English — let's talk about that

People underestimate what it's like to listen to regular people who might not even be native speakers. If you haven't dealt with interesting accents yet, you are in for a treat.

Someone doing a tutorial speaks at average speed and pronounces everything correctly. Real life is not like that. It happens with native speakers in their own language. It happens everywhere.

You'll work with people from different countries, and some of them don't care much about pronunciation as long as they can explain what they mean. That works both ways.

### Explaining your ideas is key

This applies both in interviews and in building good relationships with clients. Not just through speaking or writing. You can use complementary tools to support yourself:

- **Diagrams:** an architecture diagram is worth more than a thousand words in a technical interview.
- **Digital whiteboards:** Miro, Excalidraw, whatever works.
- **Grammarly:** can help with your writing as a complementary resource.

## Preparation makes the difference

At some point you'll be able to walk into interviews without much preparation. But until then, prepare.

It's not just about studying. It's about practicing how you talk about your experience. It's about having clear answers to the obvious questions. It's about knowing what you want to convey and having a plan to do it.

The next time someone asks "tell me about yourself" in an interview, you shouldn't be thinking about what to say. You should be choosing which version of your story to tell.
